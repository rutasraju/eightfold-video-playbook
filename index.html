The Video Storytelling Playbook

Show the work,
not the software.

A shared standard for how Eightfold tells product stories on film — for Cultivate, launches, and every moment in between. Outcome-led. Human-first. AI as the quiet engine, never the headline.

Audience
Design, Product, Marketing

— North Star

If we removed the UI, would the story still make sense?

The single test that governs every video we make.
01/06

Three principles.
Non-negotiable.

Most playbooks have ten rules nobody remembers. Ours has three, ranked. The first one is the one we forget first under deadline.

PRINCIPLE 01

The human moment is non-optional.

Every Eightfold video must contain at least one moment of empathy — a candidate taking a break, a recruiter being shielded from noise, a manager being given clarity. This is what separates our category POV from Workday, SAP, and the rest. It is the thing that makes the work feel designed, not just built.

→ Show the pause, the help, the relief. One per film, minimum.
PRINCIPLE 02

AI is felt, not seen.

Every competitor is making "look how smart our AI is" videos right now. Our differentiation is the opposite: AI disappears into the workflow. Show the lift, not the model. Show what changes for the human, not what's happening in the system. The hero is always the person, never the algorithm.

→ If the AI is on screen more than the human, restart.
PRINCIPLE 03

One outcome.
One story.

The fastest way to ruin a product film is to demonstrate everything the product can do. Pick one outcome. Tell one journey. Resist every instinct to add "and it also does X." Feature breadth belongs in datasheets — not in the film that has 90 seconds to make someone feel something.

→ One persona, one journey, one takeaway.
02/06

Before you write
a single line.

Two questions must be answered before any script begins. They are the difference between a film that feels designed and one that feels assembled.

— Step 0.1

Name the one person.

Not "users." Not "customers." Not "the TA team." A specific Bersin segment with a specific job to be done. Talent Acquisition leader at a 5,000-person enterprise drowning in unqualified applications. Hiring manager who sees twenty resumes a week and trusts none of them. CHRO who needs to defend an AI decision to a board.

If you can describe them in one sentence, you can write for them. If you can't, the script will drift.

GATE: Persona on the page in one sentence, or stop.
— Step 0.2

Design the cold open.

The first three seconds determine whether anyone watches the next eighty-seven. A logo card, a title slate, a slow zoom into a dashboard — these are how you lose the room before you've spoken. Open inside the human's world: their inbox at 7am, the stack of resumes, the meeting they're dreading.

The opening frame is the thesis. Treat it that way.

GATE: Frame one is a moment, not a logo.
03/06

The seven beats
of an Eightfold film.

Every video follows this spine. The middle beat — the experience story — is where most films live or die. Spend it on one journey, deeply. Not five journeys, shallowly.

BEAT 01 ~10 seconds

The tension.

Set context inside the human's day

Open in the world as it is. Not chaos for chaos's sake — a recognizable, specific moment of friction. The thing this person is tired of doing.

Script template
"Every Monday, [persona] opens [tool] to find [recognizable problem]."
BEAT 02 ~7 seconds

The shift.

Open the door — create curiosity

Pivot from "this is how it is" to "this is how it could be." A visual collapse, a simplification, a moment of suggestion. Don't reveal the product yet.

Script template
"What if the work itself got easier — not the workaround?"
BEAT 03 ~5 seconds

The introduction.

Name the product. Cleanly.

One bold reveal. No tagline soup. The product name, and one line that frames it as a category move — never a feature dump.

Script template
"This is [Product]. The [category] reimagined for [outcome]."
BEAT 04 ~45 seconds · the heart

The journey.

One person, one path, end-to-end

The film lives or dies here. Pick one user. Pick one job. Walk it from entry to completion in three to four steps maximum. Seamless transitions. No tab-switching narration. This is where the human moment lands — the pause, the guidance, the moment of relief.

Beat structure
Entry → Onboarding → Core action → Human moment → Completion
BEAT 05 ~12 seconds

The quiet engine.

Show AI doing the lifting — subtly

This is where most competitors get loud. We get quiet. A subtle visual of scoring, summarizing, ranking — happening in the background while the human stays the focus.

Script template
"Behind the scenes, [system] handles [hard thing] — so [persona] doesn't have to."
BEAT 06 ~18 seconds

The outcome.

Translate experience into value

Compress time. Show before and after. Three outcome words, max. Faster. Clearer. Truer. Resist the urge to list every benefit — pick the one that lands hardest for this persona.

Script template
"What used to take [X] now takes [Y]. And the work is better."
BEAT 07 ~5 seconds

The landing.

Memorable. Forward-looking. Earned.

One line that elevates the product into a category statement. Not a slogan — a thesis. Land it on a still frame, not a logo animation.

Script template
"This isn't just [product]. It's a new way to [verb the category]."
04/06

How it should
look and sound.

Visual restraint and language restraint are the same discipline. Both come from picking one thing per scene, per sentence, and trusting it to carry the weight.

Do

  • One key moment per scene. If two things matter equally, you have two scenes.
  • Motion that simplifies. Use animation to collapse complexity, never to add ornament.
  • Clean, minimal UI. Real product, real data, generous whitespace.
  • Progression you can feel. Each cut moves the human forward. No sideways scenes.
  • Outcome language. "You see the right candidates faster."
  • Speak from where the category is going. Position with confidence, not against incumbents.

Don't

  • Feature stacking. "And it also does…" is the death of every film.
  • Busy screens. If the viewer has to choose what to look at, you've already lost.
  • Long on-screen text. If they're reading, they're not feeling.
  • Context jumps. Tab-hopping breaks the spell. Pick one journey, stay in it.
  • Capability language. "AI evaluates candidates using multiple signals."
  • Anti-incumbent framing. "Current systems are broken" is a challenger move. We're not a challenger anymore.
— LANGUAGE SHIFTS
AI evaluates candidates using multiple signals
becomes →
You see the right candidates — faster.
Our platform unifies fragmented HR systems
becomes →
One place. One source. One decision.
Current talent acquisition tools are broken
becomes →
This is the next evolution of how teams hire.
Leverage advanced ML to screen at scale
becomes →
Trust the shortlist. Spend time where it matters.
05/06

The thirty-second
compression test.

Before any script gets approved, it has to pass this. If you can't tell the story in thirty seconds, you don't have a story yet — you have a list of features wearing a costume.

30
Seconds. Out loud. No edits.

Tell the whole film in one breath.

Pull up the script. Read the story aloud — not the bullets, the actual narrative arc. Time yourself. Thirty seconds, max.

If you can't, one of three things is true: you're telling more than one story, you don't know which outcome leads, or your AI is the protagonist when it should be the supporting cast.

This test is not optional. Every reviewer should ask for it. Every writer should have it ready.

06/06

Three gates.
Pass all three.

Before any video ships, it must pass these three checks. Not seven. Not twelve. Three. If it fails one, it goes back — no exceptions, no compromise on deadline.

1

One outcome,
one story.

Can a non-product person describe the film in a single sentence? If they list two things, the film is two films. Send it back.

2

One human
moment.

Find the moment of empathy on the timeline. Point to it. If you can't point to it, it isn't there. Add it before approval.

3

AI as support,
not subject.

Count seconds where the human is on screen vs. seconds where the system is. The human should win, decisively. If not, recut.

FAIL ANY ONE → BACK TO THE WRITER, NOT FORWARD TO PRODUCTION.
Craft is the gate. Polish is craft. We don't approve videos that need to be "fixed in editing."
