00/07

What is the job
of this video?

Before any audience, before any storyboard, before any screen recording — name what this video is for. The rules in this playbook are tuned for design's concept videos. Apply them to a marketing video or a sales demo and you'll make those worse, not better.

Video type Primary question it answers This playbook?
Concept finalization "Is this the design we're building?" Yes — apply fully
Vision exploration "What could this become?" Yes — same spine, more concept UI
Internal design pitch "Why should we invest in this?" Yes — sharpen the ask
Marketing video "Why should I care?" No — Marketing adapts from ours
Sales / product demo "How does this actually work?" No — owned by Sales/Product
Customer story "Has this worked for someone like me?" No — interview-led
Explainer "Can you help me understand this concept?" No — concept-led, not design-led
GATE: If this isn't a design concept video, stop reading. Marketing videos are made by Marketing — often from one of ours (see Chapter 07). Sales demos are owned by Sales. Each format has its own playbook and its own rules; ours will make them worse.
— TWO MODES INSIDE THE SCOPE

Concept finalization sells what's about to ship. Higher real-UI ratio. The ask is usually engineering capacity, prioritization, or sign-off. Vision exploration sells what could exist. Concept UI throughout. The ask is alignment, funding, or appetite for the exploration. Same seven-beat spine; different fidelity, different ask.

Shown to customers? Sometimes — as a thinking artifact in initial discovery conversations, never as a roadmap commitment. Frame verbally before playing ("here's how design is thinking about this"). Keep the concept chyron visible. Never hand off the file — Marketing handles any external distribution.

Upgrade path. A concept video can graduate to a sales-grade demo video — after Leadership reviews and approves. Cultivate is the canonical example: what starts as design-led storytelling becomes the demo the Sales team takes to customers. The video doesn't change; the audience does. See Chapter 07.

01/07

Three principles.
Non-negotiable.

Most playbooks have ten rules nobody remembers. Ours has three, ranked. The first one is the one we break first under deadline pressure.

PRINCIPLE 01

One idea.
One journey.

The fastest way to lose the room is to pitch three concepts in one video. Pick the one the audience needs to see. Walk it end-to-end. Resist every instinct to add "and it also does X" — adjacent ideas dilute the central one, and the decision you came in for becomes a debate you didn't plan for.

→ If the audience can't repeat the concept back in one sentence, the video has more than one concept in it.
PRINCIPLE 02

Honest about fidelity.

Concept videos legitimately mix shipping UI with prototype frames and pure vision. That's the point — design's job is to make the future tangible before it's built. But every frame must be honestly labeled. The fastest way to burn internal credibility is to imply something ships when it doesn't, or to imply something's a sketch when it's already in production.

→ "Shipping" · "In build" · "Concept" · "Vision" — pick the chyron, keep it visible.
PRINCIPLE 03

Designed enough to elevate.

Polish is the point. A concept video should pull the room's conversation up to the idea, not down to the fidelity. If stakeholders are critiquing the typography, the typography is doing too little to disappear. If they're arguing about the concept, the video is working. The craft is invisible when it's right.

→ If the feedback is about the polish, the polish isn't carrying the idea.
02/07

Before you open
the storyboard.

Two questions must be answered before any script begins. They are the difference between a concept video that lands a decision and one that becomes a meeting unto itself.

— Step 0.1

Name the room.

Not "leadership." Not "the team." A specific group with a specific decision to make. The CPO and engineering leads who need to greenlight Q3 capacity. The exec staff who are deciding whether to invest in this category at all. A target customer who's evaluating us against a competitor and needs to see we're thinking three steps ahead.

If you can describe the room in one sentence, you can pitch into it. If you can't, the video will pitch to no one in particular — and land nowhere.

GATE: Room on the page in one sentence, or stop.
— Step 0.2

Name the ask.

Not "feedback." Not "alignment." A specific action you want the room to take after watching. Approve this concept for build in the next sprint. Commit two engineers for an eight-week prototype. Endorse this direction at next week's board meeting.

The ask shapes everything: the length, the fidelity, the language, the final beat. A concept video without an ask is a sketch with sound — engaging, but it won't move anything.

GATE: One ask, named in one sentence with a verb in it, or stop.
03/07

The seven beats
of a concept video.

Every design concept video follows this spine. The middle beat — the experience — is where the concept gets made tangible. Spend it on one journey, deeply. Mix real and concept UI freely, but label every frame.

BEAT 01 ~25 seconds

The opportunity.

Why this matters now

Open in the specific friction or possibility the concept addresses. Not chaos, not generalities — a named gap in how things work today, or a named shift in the category that opens a door. The room needs to feel the stakes before they evaluate the answer.

Script template
"Today, [persona] spends [N hours] doing [task] manually — and the AI we already shipped can do most of it. The question is what the next layer looks like."
BEAT 02 ~25 seconds

The thinking.

The point of view behind the concept

Name the design POV. The principle, the framing, the bet the concept rests on. This is the most-skipped beat — and the one that earns the right to be wrong about specifics. If the room buys the thinking, the details become collaborative.

Script template
"We started from one principle: [POV]. If that's right, then the experience needs to [behavior] — not [common assumption]."
BEAT 03 ~20 seconds

The concept reveal.

Name it. Cleanly.

One frame that introduces the concept. Give it a name the room can use after the meeting ends. Concepts that don't get named don't get talked about.

Script template
"We're calling it [name]. The idea is [one-line description] — here's what it feels like."
BEAT 04 ~2 minutes · the experience

The journey.

One person, one path, end-to-end

The video lives or dies here. Pick one user. Pick one moment in their work. Walk it through the concept end-to-end. Real UI where it exists; concept UI where it doesn't; vision frames where neither does. Every frame labeled. The labeling is the trust contract — break it once and the rest of the video pays for it.

Beat structure
Entry → Action → Designed moment → Result · (every frame labeled real / concept / vision)
BEAT 05 ~30 seconds

The intelligence.

How the AI behaves in this concept

Show the AI's role honestly — what's autonomous, what's assisted, where the human stays in the loop. Don't hide it (the room is evaluating it). Don't oversell it (the room will believe you, then hold you to it). Designed AI behavior is a feature; show the design.

Script template
"The system handles [X] autonomously. [Persona] stays in control of [Y]. Here's the handoff moment — that's where the design choice is."
BEAT 06 ~25 seconds

The unlock.

What this enables that wasn't possible before

Land the consequence of the concept. Not a list of features — the new thing the user (or the business, or the category) can do because this exists. Compress time; show the delta; let the unlock be the argument for the ask.

Script template
"With this in place, [persona] can [new behavior] — which means [business consequence]. That's what we couldn't do before."
BEAT 07 ~20 seconds · the ask

The ask.

What we need from the room to take this forward

Every internal concept video ends with an ask. Not a CTA — an ask. Name it, scope it, make it actionable in the meeting that follows. Videos that end on aesthetics waste the decision the room is ready to make.

Script template
"To take this forward, we need [specific resource / decision / sign-off] by [when]. The next meeting on the calendar is the right place to commit."
04/07

How it should
look and sound.

Concept-video restraint sits between marketing's emotional polish and a demo's UI rigor. The fidelity is the message — too rough and the room argues the visuals; too pixel-perfect and the room mistakes concept for commitment. Find the line, label every frame.

Do

  • Label fidelity in every frame. "Shipping," "In build," "Concept," "Vision" — pick the chyron and keep it visible.
  • Voice the design POV. Narrate the thinking, not just the action. "We chose this because..." earns more than "and then..."
  • Designed cuts. Concept videos are allowed cinema — meaningful transitions, considered pacing, intentional silence.
  • Real moments where possible. Anchor concept frames between real ones. The mix is what gives the concept credibility.
  • Name the concept. Give the idea a handle the room can use after the meeting ends.
  • Show the design seams. Where the human takes over from the AI. Where the model is uncertain. Where the user can override. The seams are the design.

Don't

  • Imply roadmap. Concept frames without labels read as commitments. One un-labeled frame can become a renegotiation.
  • Pitch three ideas at once. Adjacent concepts collapse the central one. Pick one; spin the others off as their own videos.
  • Over-polish the AI. Showing the model as omniscient sets internal expectations you'll have to walk back later.
  • Skip the thinking. If you go straight to the experience without the POV, the room debates pixels instead of principles.
  • End on aesthetics. The final frame must carry an ask. A logo and a fade is a meeting that won't decide anything.
  • Forget the chyron. Especially if a customer might see it. One missing label is a misalignment incident waiting to happen.

— THE FIDELITY CHYRON

Every concept video uses one of four labels in the top-left of every frame. The label is small, persistent, and non-decorative — it's a credibility contract with the audience, not a visual flourish.

SHIPPING
means →
In production today. Real product, real data.
IN BUILD
means →
Engineering has it. Specifics may shift; the workflow won't.
CONCEPT
means →
Design intent. Not yet committed to build. Open to iteration.
VISION
means →
Future-state thinking. No commitment implied or read.
05/07

The one-watch
playback test.

Before any concept video gets approved, it has to pass this. If a stakeholder can't repeat the concept back after one watch, the video has done the room a disservice — it filled time without landing an idea, and the meeting that follows will be a debate about what they think they saw.

1
Watch. Then summary back. No prompting.

Play it once. Ask what they saw.

Pull a colleague who isn't on the project. Show the video once. Don't introduce it. Don't narrate over it. When it ends, ask: what's the concept, and what are we being asked to do?

If they hedge — "something about agents..." or "the AI does more stuff?" — one of three things is true: the concept doesn't have a name yet, the ask is buried, or the video is pitching more than one idea.

This test is not optional. Every reviewer should run it. Every designer should have a colleague-pair lined up before the cut goes to the executive room. Concept videos that fail the playback test fail in the meeting that follows.

06/07

Three gates.
Pass all three.

Before any concept video ships, it must pass these three checks. Not seven. Not twelve. Three. If it fails one, it goes back — no exceptions, no compromise on the executive meeting on the calendar.

1

One idea,
cleanly stated.

One watch, one sentence back — no prompting. If the stakeholder hedges or lists two things, the concept is fuzzy or the video has more than one idea in it. Recut before the room sees it.

2

Honest about
fidelity.

Every frame carries one of the four chyrons (Shipping · In Build · Concept · Vision). Scrub the cut. Find a frame without one. If you find one, that frame doesn't ship — it's a credibility leak in a video about credibility.

3

The ask is
unmistakable.

After the final beat, can the room name the specific action, the specific people, and the specific date? If the answer is "share feedback," the ask isn't sharp enough to move anything. Rewrite Beat 07 and re-cut.

FAIL ANY ONE → BACK TO THE DESIGNER, NOT FORWARD TO THE EXECUTIVE ROOM.
Craft is the gate. Concept clarity is craft. We don't ship videos that need a designer in the room to be understood.
07/07

Lifecycle
& handoff.

Every concept video lives in stages. Product and Design produce it; Leadership reviews it for sales-grade promotion; Marketing adapts it for public distribution. Different hands, different bars, different definitions of "done" at each step. A video doesn't move forward without the previous stage signing off — and not every video needs to.

1

Production.

Joint output of Product and Design. Used to align internal leadership and to spark initial discovery conversations with customers. Lives inside Eightfold until Stage 2 promotes it — or stays a concept artifact, which is also a fine outcome.

— STAGE 3 DETAIL: THE HANDOFF KIT
Hand over
→
Editable source files (Figma frames, video master). Raw narration script and audio. Asset list with fidelity chyrons intact. One-page brief on the concept, the internal ask achieved, and who signed off.
Keep
→
The POV. The narrative arc (opportunity → thinking → concept → unlock). The fidelity choices and what each frame represents. If Marketing changes these, the concept stops being ours.
Let go
→
Brand intros and outros. Music, transitions, polish. VO swapped for public-facing voice. Internal-only context removed (no team names, no deadlines, no deal references). Length and CTAs adjusted per channel.
CUSTOMER-CONVERSATION ADDENDUM →
